## Technique Sessions

### Technique 1: First Principles Thinking

**Objective:** Strip away pitch deck assumptions and rebuild from verified fundamental truths.

**Verified Truths Established:**

**Corporate ESG Side:**
- CSRD/LkSG/CSDDD/Taxonomy regulations are real and in force with staggered deadlines
- BUT regulatory direction uncertain - "stop-the-clock" postponements + Omnibus package aims to REDUCE burden by 25%
- Wave 1 companies (500+ employees) are reporting NOW (2025) - already found solutions
- Wave 2/3 companies have 3-4+ years before mandatory reporting

**Gen Z/Student Side:**
- Gen Z has LOWEST formal volunteering rate (20-25%) of any generation
- Gen Z LEAST likely to help neighbors informally
- Millennials (ages 27-42) showed largest volunteering gains, NOT Gen Z
- When Gen Z does volunteer: needs tangible impact visibility, career/skills benefits, values alignment
- What DOESN'T motivate Gen Z: social aspects, "feels good to help" (same as all generations)

**Two-Sided Marketplace Economics:**
- Most two-sided marketplaces FAIL at chicken-and-egg problem
- Liquidity is the keystone - without it, there's no marketplace
- Must solve for HARDER side first, then easier side follows
- Critical mass required BEFORE network effects kick in

**CRITICAL INSIGHT - The Uncomfortable Truth:**

Based ONLY on verified facts, there isn't an urgent ESG reporting problem that SMEs will pay to solve NOW because:
- Wave 1 companies already found solutions
- Wave 2/3 have 3-4 years to figure it out
- Regulations being postponed and potentially reduced by 25%
- Gen Z has LOWEST volunteering rate and LEAST community engagement

**The two-sided marketplace fails first principles:**
- Companies aren't urgently seeking this (3-4 years runway)
- Gen Z isn't seeking this (lowest engagement rates)
- You'd need both sides to exist before either side gets value

**Alternative Problem Identified:**
"Wave 1 companies (500+ employees) need ONGOING operational efficiency for their EXISTING ESG reporting processes"
- âœ… Urgent NOW (reporting in 2025)
- âœ… Paying TODAY (proven willingness to pay)
- âœ… One-sided (no marketplace needed, B2B SaaS)
- âœ… Specific pain (existing solutions likely expensive/inefficient)

**Key Realization:** "It is really hard to find a pressing, monetizable problem" with the current SolvTerra concept.

### Technique 2: Five Whys (Root Cause Analysis)

**Objective:** Drill past surface problems to uncover the ROOT motivations and consequences.

**PATH A: Corporate Side - "Companies need verified ESG data"**

**WHY #1:** Why do companies need verified ESG data?
- Financial penalties for non-compliance (CSRD violations up to â‚¬10M or 5% of turnover)
- Exclusion from public contracts (especially under CSDDD)
- Access to capital - Banks/investors increasingly require ESG disclosure

**WHY #2:** Why do companies care about these penalties/exclusions/capital access?
- Loss of revenue streams (public contracts = significant % for many companies)
- Higher cost of capital (ESG laggards pay higher interest rates)
- Competitive disadvantage (competitors with better ESG access cheaper funding)

**WHY #3:** Why does losing competitive advantage/paying more for capital matter?
- Survival threshold: In competitive markets, higher operating costs = potential business failure
- Shareholder value: Board/executive compensation tied to stock performance
- Market position: Exclusion from growing ESG-conscious customer segments

**WHY #4:** Why does market position/survival matter to decision-makers?
- Personal financial security (job loss, equity value)
- Professional reputation (career prospects)
- Legal liability (directors can be personally liable for ESG failures in some jurisdictions)

**WHY #5 - THE ROOT:**
â†’ **FEAR OF PERSONAL CONSEQUENCES**

The executives making ESG decisions ultimately fear:
- Personal financial loss
- Career damage
- Legal exposure

**ðŸŽ¯ CRITICAL INSIGHT - The Real Problem:**
Companies don't actually want ESG data. They want **PERSONAL RISK MITIGATION**.

The root problem isn't "we need ESG reporting" - it's **"I could personally lose my job/money/reputation if we screw this up"**

**Implication:** Executives buy personal risk insurance, not ESG reporting tools.

**PATH B: Student Side - "Students want to volunteer"**

**WHY #1:** Why WOULD a Gen Z student complete a micro-sustainability challenge (given most DON'T volunteer)?
- Career advancement - Skills/experience for resume (verified motivator)
- Tangible recognition - Certificates, badges, LinkedIn credentials
- Financial incentive - Even small rewards (observation: "doesn't have to be money" implies something of value)

**WHY #2:** Why do they need career advancement/recognition/rewards?
- Job market competition - Entry-level requires "experience" even for first jobs
- Student debt pressure - Need to maximize employability
- Differentiation anxiety - Everyone has similar degrees, need standout factors

**WHY #3:** Why does employability/differentiation matter so urgently to them?
- Cost of living crisis (rent, food, expenses rising faster than entry wages)
- Gig economy reality (less job security than previous generations)
- Social comparison pressure (LinkedIn/social media makes peer success visible)

**WHY #4:** Why does economic security matter more than "doing good"?
- Precarious financial position - Can't afford to work for free
- Hierarchy of needs - Survival before self-actualization
- Pragmatic generation - Witnessed 2008, COVID, multiple "once-in-a-lifetime" crises

**WHY #5 - THE ROOT:**
â†’ **FEAR OF ECONOMIC IRRELEVANCE**

Gen Z will engage IF and ONLY IF it directly helps them avoid:
- Unemployment/underemployment
- Financial insecurity
- Being outcompeted by peers

**ðŸŽ¯ CRITICAL INSIGHT - The Real Problem:**
Gen Z doesn't want to volunteer. They want **COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGE IN A BRUTAL JOB MARKET**.

The root problem isn't "how do we get students to help with ESG" - it's **"How do we help students not fall behind economically?"**

**Implication:** Students will only engage if it directly improves employability and career prospects.

---

**ðŸ’¥ THE FUNDAMENTAL DISCONNECT:**

**What Executives Actually Want:** Personal risk mitigation - bulletproof coverage from ESG failures

**What Students Actually Want:** Competitive advantage in job market - employability and economic security

**What SolvTerra Offers:** Micro-volunteering challenges that generate ESG data points

**The Gap:**
- Does crowdsourced micro-volunteering data actually reduce executive personal risk? (Questionable - auditors may not accept it)
- Does completing micro-challenges actually improve student employability? (Unlikely - "I picked up trash for eco-points" isn't a resume builder)

**The brutal realization:** The original value proposition may not solve either side's ROOT problem.
